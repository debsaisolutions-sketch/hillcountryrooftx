Hail Damage Inspection After Hill Country Storms: What Homeowners Should Know

Texas Hill Country storms can drop hail that bruises shingles, dents metal panels, and cracks ridge caps—even when the house looks fine from the driveway. Damage that is not repaired can open the door to leaks, attic moisture, and insurance headaches later. A careful post-storm check helps you catch problems early.

What Hail Does to a Roof

Hail impact varies by size, wind, and roof material:

  • Asphalt shingles: Soft or darkened spots, granule loss, bruised mats, and cracked tabs.
  • Metal roofing: Dents, coating chips, and fastener stress that can start corrosion over time.
  • Flashings and vents: Bent pipe boots, loose step flashing, and damaged ridge vents are easy to miss.
  • Gutters and soft metals: Dented gutters and downspouts can point to the same storm that hit the roof.

Signs You Can Spot From the Ground

Stay off the roof after a storm. From the yard or a second-story window, look for:

  • Missing, curled, or uneven shingles
  • Granules collecting in gutters or downspout splash areas
  • Dented gutters, AC fins, or soft metal trim (a clue hail was large enough to matter)
  • New wet spots in the attic, ceiling stains, or dripping after the next rain
  • Loose or crooked ridge caps and vent covers

If you see those signs—or a neighbor’s roof was claimed as hail-damaged—schedule a professional inspection even if you do not have an active leak yet.

Why a Pro Inspection Matters for Insurance

Insurers often want documented storm damage: photos, material notes, and a clear scope of repair vs. replacement. A licensed roofer can identify impact bruises that are hard to see from the ground, check valleys and penetrations, and help you understand whether a patch or a larger restoration makes sense. Document the storm date and take your own exterior photos before cleanup if it is safe to do so.

What Not to Do

  1. Do not walk the roof after hail. Wet or damaged surfaces are slippery and can worsen broken shingles.
  2. Do not delay forever. Small openings can turn into interior damage with the next heavy rain.
  3. Do not assume “no leak = no damage.” Impact bruises can shorten shingle life before water shows up inside.

After the Inspection

Repairs may be as simple as replacing a few damaged shingles and boots—or as involved as a partial or full re-roof if bruising is widespread. Metal and shingle systems handle Texas heat differently; your roofer can also talk through material options if replacement is on the table.
